CASE STUDY - MIND MAP


Roles : UX Researcher & UX Designer
Team : Anna Gombay
This case study represents a large, independent project that I am currently working on. My intention is to design and develop a mobile application that supports prescription medication users' efforts to maintain their mental health.

The app will feature:
(1) a unique and comprehensive mood rating scale
(2) medication tracking/reminders

The primary goal is to provide users with insight into how their medication regime correlates with their own self-reported mental health over time.

Introduction
Were you born sad?

It’s somewhat of an absurd question, but one I found myself facing the first time a doctor offered to prescribe me antidepressants. It had never occurred to me that the way I felt wasn’t standard, or that my perception of existence was warped enough to require medical intervention.
It turns out it was.

This study isnt about me, though. It is dedicated to the people who still can't pick up the phone. To those who face overwhelming dread of waking up, or feel that life takes more effort than it is worth.

Because no, this should not be the standard.

The Problem
So, what is the standard experience of a prescription medication user? Is the product's intended benefit-- the improvement of well-being-- reliably present?

To answer this question, let's focus on the existing knowledge we have of the relationship between prescription medication use and self-rated mood.
Statistics Canada indicates that:

(a) 41% percent of Canadians are prescription medication users

(b) 60% of this cohort reports poor emotional well-being

In other words, roughly 25% of our country is treatment resistant. That is, 1 in 4 Canadians are on prescription medication and remain generally unhappy.

User Interviews
My objective with user interviewing was to understand how taking medication affects daily living, and to identify room for improvement in the treatment process. I asked the following:

(1) Which symptoms are you seeking to treat by using prescription medication?

(2) Has taking prescription medication changed your daily life? If yes, how so?
(3) Do you ever forget to take your medication? If so, how often?

(4) How do you think technology could play a role in assisting you with your medication use?

(5) Have you ever discontinued medication use in the past? If yes, why?

(6) How would you rate your daily mood on 0-5 scale?
After conducting five interviews, I began to string together common themes in my findings.

Concentrating on potential user goals, needs, roles, and interests, I then formed rough, data-driven profiles of three prospective clients.

Upon iteration, these profiles were ultimately transformed into high-fidelity user personas.

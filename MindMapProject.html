This case study represents a large, independent project that I am currently working on. My intention is to design and develop a mobile application that supports prescription medication users' efforts to maintain their mental health.

The app will feature a unique and comprehensive mood rating scale, medication tracking/reminders, and a daily journal that allows users to reflect on their thoughts.

Other app details and specifications are currently under review.

TL;DR
> 1 in 4 Canadians are on prescription medication and feel generally unhappy

> I’m designing and developing a mobile application to help improve the well-being of this population

> The mobile application will measure users’ medication intake, mood, energy levels, and thoughts

Did you know that 41% of Canadians aged 6-79 use prescription medication? Of this cohort, 60% frequently report poor emotional well-being. This means that 1 in 4 Canadians are on prescription medication and feel generally unhappy. Such a statistic does not sit well with me, so I’ve designed a way to help.

Inspired by a desire to improve the outcome of mental health treatment, Mind Map provides users with objective feedback surrounding the way in which particular medications affect their mood, energy levels and thoughts. My hope is that this insight will assist users in choosing the most suitable prescription medications for their unique needs.

It should be noted that this application is in no way a subsitute for clinical advice, rather it is a tool that can help clients and practitioners evaluate the efficacy of a given treatment plan.

The first step in my UX process involved exploring existing mobile applications with features similar to those I had in mind. I knew that I needed to examine three specific variables:

- medication use
- mood tracking
- journaling

To prepare for my analysis I compiled a list of nine mobile applications, each of which met a predetermined criteria.* I then installed each application on my phone and explored the user experience, remaining mindful of components such as the app’s aesthetics, its level of user engagement, the user flow, potential pain points, etc.

Taking notes, I created a 12-point feedback grid for each application, dividing my data into the following four categories:

- likes
- criticisms
- questions
- ideas

After data-collection, I was left with 108 datapoints to play around with. Feel free to interactively explore my findings below!

Apps that featured positive usability gave power to the user; these designs afforded users with the opportunity to correct mistakes (e.g. accidental button presses) and were laid out in an intuitive, efficient manner.

My data, however, reveals a relatively high proportion of usability issues in contrast to positive user experiences. The most notable criticism that emerged during my analysis was the amount of time and effort it took to use certain apps (particularly those involving medication). Whether it was due to poor structural design, vague instructions, or non-skippable animations, these applications seemed to risk leaving users frustrated during the navigation process.

Expanding on the notion of poor structural design-- many questions arose regarding the layout and function of app elements. For instance, some applications featured an overwhelming number of home screen buttons, potentially leaving users uncertain of which option to prioritize. Further, certain elements did not carry out their tagged functions (e.g. edit feature on journal does not work > user must re-submit entire entry to include missed data), signalling questions surrounding the usability of the written code.

In terms of fixing usability errors, several ideas come to mind: designers can ensure that homescreens are evident and accessible to the user by chunking priority items into 3-4 categories, developers can make data entry more interactive so that users don't feel as though they are filling out paperwork in a clinic, and--importantly-- always give the user power to skip through undesired sections of text or animation. Time is precious; designers must respect this.

After gaining a valuable array of quantitative data through secondary research, I began collecting qualitative data--the information that guides user experience designers to create truly human-centered products.

To begin learning about my prospective users, I conducted a series of five interviews during the months of December 2019 and January 2020. Each user was screened to fit a predetermined critera*, and voluntarily answered the following six questions:

1. Which symptoms are you seeking to treat by using prescription medication?

2. How has taking prescription medication changed your daily life?

3. Do you ever forget to take your medication? If so, how often?

4. How do you think technology could play a role in assisting you with your medication use?

5. Have you ever discontinued medication use in the past? If yes, why?

6. How would you rate your daily mood on the following scale?

1 - very bad
2 - bad
3 - neutral
4 - good
5 - very good


* All participants were smart phone users, and had been taking prescription medication for at least three months as of the date of their interview.

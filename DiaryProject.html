DIARY STUDY - AUDIO EXPERIENCES


Roles : UX Researcher
Team : Anna Gombay, Mickey Xiong, Aporva Bhardwaj
This one-month diary study was completed as part of an Empathic Research Frameworks course at Humber College. Our goal was to capture the thoughts, behaviours, and emotions of users as they engage in audio experiences.

Examples of an audio experience include time spent listening to music, podcasts, or audiobooks. For this project, our group chose to focus on the user experience of streaming music on a mobile phone.

Introduction
Whether experienced as a passive delight or a nostalgic mental excursion, music has the ability to arouse emotion in its listeners.

Some may even say that music serves as an accessible form of emotion regulation, given that its digitization has made it so readily available; all it takes is one tap to become immersed in a new world.

The creation of a touchpoint linking humans to the world of music has initiated a competition within the market, urging streaming services to develop progressively more unique methods of deliverance. Prevalent companies such as Google, Apple, and Spotify are consistently adjusting their algorithms in an attempt to attract the largest user audiences.

Data Collection
Except for day one, each participant was to answer three questions, once a day, for three days straight. We collected our data through Google Forms, asking the following questions:

1) What is your favourite and most consistently used feature on your music streaming application?

2) When did you find yourself using the feature most frequently today? Morning, afternoon, or evening?

3) Has the feature changed your mood today? If yes, was the change positive or negative?

4) If you could make changes to your experience with the feature today, what would it be?

Data Analysis
As a group, we reviewed our participants’ responses to determine whether any data should be excluded. While our participants did properly follow their given instructions, we discovered that an error had been made on our part-- instead of sending a single prompt per-person for question one as intended, we sent three.
This error meant that instead of reporting on a single feature, our respondents were able to choose up to three. To remedy the issue, we focused on the nine responses below and grouped them in terms of similarity.

Ideally, we would have been able to create a 3x3 matrix, but we were happy to see that for the most part, participants remained consistent with their reporting:
User Responses to Initial Research Prompt

Data Synthesis
After agreeing that there were three distinct data-points that captured our users’ responses, our team excluded six erroneous entries. With a clean dataset, we were now ready to begin sorting through our users’ responses to synthesize the information. For this diary study, we organized our data using an affinity mapping technique.

To begin, we each composed insights and common themes that arose from our analyses. Then, using sticky notes, we pasted our findings on a board and collectively reviewed them.

One note at a time, we shared whether we had recorded similar insights until we were left with a cluster of four themes, and two outliers:
Users desire custom music recommendations Users are frustrated that they can't access music offline Users access their music apps day and night Listening to music generally improves mood Outliers

Expanding on Themes
As a final step in the diary study process, I spent time expanding on the themes we had previously identified as a group.

Below, I explore our respondents' feedback in greater detail, reporting on implications of the desires, frustrations, behaviours, and emotions involved in the music streaming experience.
Users desire custom music recommendations Users are frustrated that they can't access music offline Users access their music apps day and night Listening to music generally improves mood Outliers

Many of our participants voiced a predilection for applications that compute music suggestions based on existing libraries. Such a feature could save time for users wanting to access familiar music without mental exertion. Companies thus may benefit from strengthening the scope and validity of their suggestive algorithms.

Reflection
This project taught our team that even on a small scale, user data can provide meaningful feedback and inspire avenues for novel future design.

We learned (the hard way) the importance of planning and this obstacle presented our team with an opportunity for collaborative problem solving to protect the validity of our study.
If given the opportunity to start over from the beginning, our team would likely craft our research questions more carefully.

It may have been helpful to spend more time reflecting first on how we, ourselves, would have answered the questions, and what kind of insight these answers could provide.
For instance, we asked users what time of day they use their favourite feature most frequently without considering why we were asking this question.

Focusing too much on high level concepts included in the research prompt (i.e. explore the “user behaviour”) may have foiled our ability to truly take the user into account during the preparation process.
